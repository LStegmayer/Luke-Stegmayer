A Custom NES Palette

Creating a deliberately inaccurate, hopefully more pleasing picture

August 6th, 2022

This is my palette. There are many like it, but this one is mine.

The NES palette is a tricky thing. The PPU (Picture Processing Unit) generates the video output directly in composite, rather than RGB; as I understand it, the primary difficulty is that the NES video is out of spec, and how those out-of-spec colors are handled is undefined. There's also something to do with the properties of the integrated circuit's design and/or manufacturing, which produces unpredictable and inconsistent output. It can all get quite complicated and I don't pretend to get most of it. In any case, there can never be a single "true" or "accurate" palette. Nevertheless, people have created RGB palettes for use with emulators (both hardware and software) and NES/Famicom hardware mods, and some of them aim to closely reproduce how NES video would appear on consumer TVs or professional monitors of the day. While the word "accurate" may not apply to any NES palette, perhaps these palettes could be described as "authentic."

The work put into these authentic palettes is extensive and far beyond my knowledge. Rather, I'm aiming for the different goal of creating an NES palette that I find more aesthetically pleasing. The NES produces a range of colors that are mostly unsaturated, a bit dark, and somewhat naturalistic. This was a great strength, I suspect, of the system in its time. In contrast to the harsh, artificial primary colors of some of its competitors or home computer counterparts, the NES's palette enables convincing depictions of foliage, dirt, dimly-lit scenes, and skies that don't sear your eyes.

The "Smooth" palette by FirebrandX, one of the most popular authentic palettes.

Nevertheless, while it holds up well for its time, and I have nothing but praise for the designers of the NES PPU, there are swathes of colors here that I find unappealing. Foremost among them are the colors that the Angry Video Game Nerd liked to refer to as "puke greens", dark yellow-greens that can certainly serve a purpose, but are often used for deeply unattractive backgrounds. Additionally, the NES lacks a convincing yellow (with a dim gold being the closest), there's no bright and saturated red, and the blue-greens are too dark and saturated for my tastes.

Still, you can't make subjective improvements by just looking at a standalone palette and changing the colors you don't like. You have to look at how the colors are actually used in games. Fortunately, thanks to the features of FCEUX, it's trivial to edit the palette while playing games, so I took the approach of playing some of my favorite or otherwise popular NES games, modifying colors with HSV to make the games either resemble what arcade versions/later games/artwork indicates the artists aimed for, or otherwise simply what I like looking at. I believe I started with the "Unsaturated V6" palette, included with FCEUX, for this.

Since the various artists for these games no doubt used the same colors for different purposes, it's impossible to make a one-size-fits-all change. That said, I found that many of the alterations I made provided benefits to a surprisingly large number of games, which I attribute to the limited NES palette and common scenes/themes of NES games often constraining artists to use the same colors for the same purposes. Below, I'll go through some comparisons of these games, and I'll add a few thoughts for each one on how I feel the new palette compares, in terms of subjective appeal, to FirebrandX's rip of the NES Classic palette, because that palette is pretty authentic and because it's among the most widely-used palettes due to its creation and usage by Nintendo. Obviously, the point is not to say my palette is better than the NES Classic palette, because they're aiming for totally different goals.

Without further ado, let's start with the greatest NES game of all.

Super Mario Bros.

Off the bat, we're starting with an example where I'm explicitly going against developer intent. Miyamoto stated in an interview that he chose the sky in Super Mario Bros. to be a bit purplish. In the new palette, it's now a fresh, bright blue sky, in line with later games. The once-dull Item Blocks are now a brighter gold, and the bushes and pipes are a little brighter and less saturated now. The brown of the bricks is a little less green as well. Mario himself sports brighter, bolder red clothes and hair, arms, and shoes that are a bit less green. The end result is a game closer to the bright cartoon aesthetic of the series.

As we head underground, the primary difference is that everything is brighter and the blue-green bricks have become more of a pure blue. I like this change, but I imagine that some will feel the brighter colors are less in keeping with the dark underground theme.

In the ocean, much as above ground, the water is less purple, and the greens of the rock are a little less radioactive now.

Finally, as we head into the castle and face off against a fake Bowser, note that the lava now glows.

Balloon Fight

Leaving behind Mario for a little bit, we'll look at Balloon Fight (programmed by the legendary Satoru Iwata!). I show the title screen here to emphasize the difference in the shade of yellow used in the title; a few games use this color for titles, and I prefer the new pale yellow over the previous dull slightly greenish yellow.

Castlevania

Heading into the castle of Count Dracula, here's an example where the bright tones of the new palette may be a downside for some. The rusted red gates are looking a bit too polished. On the plus side, both the moon and Simon Belmont now have more contrast against the dark background.

Contra

The title screen of Contra is a bit of a mixed bag. I greatly prefer the new logo, but the NES Classic colors render the muscular heroes with a healthy pink flesh tone, while they're more orange with the new palette.

Getting into gameplay, there's a huge difference with the rocky cliffs; whereas before they were a sickly shade of green-brown, now they're just brown - more appealing to me, and also a greater contrast against the ground you can walk on. The trees and grass no longer glow green and appear more natural. The mountains in the background stick out a bit more, though.

Dr. Mario


I'll be frank. I think Dr. Mario's colors on the Classic are ugly, full stop. The logo, which is present at all times in gameplay, is again that sickly shade of green-yellow -- fitting, I suppose, for a game where you fight viruses, but off-putting nonetheless. Dr. Mario himself sports green-tinted hair, and the viruses are dull. With the new palette, these issues are fixed: the title is a healthy yellow, Mario has given up his imitation of the Joker, and the viruses have more contrast between them. An all-around win for the new palette, in my book.

Galaga

I don't have much to say here, but that the new palette brings the game closer to the saturated RGB colors of the original arcade game.

Pac-Man

Pac-Man's (official) NES port has a somewhat middling reputation due to its dim, dull colors, an unfortunate result of the translation from arcade to NES. With the new palette, the ghosts, dots, score text, and Pac-Man are brighter and more distinct, and thus closer to the arcade.

Super Mario Bros. 2

The game is brighter and bit less strongly saturated in the greens. The ground appears more sand-like. I've never much liked Super Mario Bros. 2's color choices, but it's helped a bit here.

Super Mario Bros. 3


The best NES game, in my book, now sports a bit bluer of a sky, brighter item boxes, and more contrast on the map screen, although the color of the paths on the map is slightly less distinct compared to the surround area now. Still, I think it's a win.

The Legend of Zelda

Hyrule now appears a bit more sun-drenched, with rivers, sand, and mountains that are much less dull. Link sports clothes made of cloth rather than nuclear waste.

Zelda II: The Adventure of Link


Zelda II was one of my motivations for this palette. The overworld is quite ugly, and the side-scrolling sections can look dull. It never fit so well with the excellent music, and I think the new palette goes a long way to making it more attractive.

Metroid

Metroid's title screen is changed quite a bit, thanks to the planet Zebes's conversion from green to brown. The planet is depicted as brown in later games, including Metroid's remake, Metroid: Zero Mission -- so it fits. Even on its own merits, I feel brown better represents a planet with a harsh, almost lifeless surface. Maybe the original artists saw the planet as being covered in moss, though. Who knows.

In game, Samus appears more like her normal self. On the other hand, maybe you prefer the darker rock tones of the Classic palette. We are underground, after all.

Jurassic Park

Jurassic Park on NES, a bit of a guilty pleasure of mine. I love the media surrounding the original film, before it received its countless middling-to-bad sequels. Needless to say, the Jurassic Park logo holds up much better with the new palette.

In game, the ground appears like dirt, rather than moss or mud. I think this is actually the intent, but I can't say for sure. The Dilo fits in better and doesn't look quite as distinctly low-color-spritish.

Mega Man 2

Mega Man's body is less greenish now, which fits in with some of his later depictions, although other later depictions retain that blue-green. The big red platforms with faces are probably a little too bright now. Win some, lose some.

Battletoads

Battletoads has always been kind of an ugly game, and this new palette doesn't fix that. Now the greenish blocks appear darker and closer to brown, but still with a green highlight, which is probably a negative change, although the enemies now stand out more against the background, which is good.

Kirby's Adventure

Kirby's Adventure is a masterclass in making the most of the NES palette, so it's not likely to see much improvement. The main difference here is that the gold color used throughout the game is brighter, which I think is a good change.

Conclusion

On the whole, I think my new palette is a big improvement over authentic palettes in terms of aesthetic appeal. Of course, people don't usually use authentic palettes for that reason, and I still think that when playing an NES/Famicom game you've never played before, or trying to understand a game's contemporary impact, or recording footage for something where such authenticity is necessary or useful, you ought to use a palette aiming to closely represent the colors generated by the console as they would have been viewed on hardware of the time. Still, when it comes to replaying games, I think I'll be using this palette in the future. It feels almost like a new coat of paint that makes returning to the classics just that much more fun.
